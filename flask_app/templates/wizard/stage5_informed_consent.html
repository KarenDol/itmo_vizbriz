Informed Consent for the Treatment of Sleep Disordered Breathing with Oral Appliances

Understanding Sleep Breathing Disorders

During sleep, breathing disorders like snoring and Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) develop when airways become narrow or blocked. While snoring results from partial airway closure producing sound, persistent heavy snoring may indicate serious health concerns including hypertension. OSA is particularly concerning as it involves complete or near-complete airway blockage throughout the night, leading to oxygen deprivation and sleep disruption. This condition can trigger various health issues including daytime fatigue, cardiac arrhythmias, hypertension, GERD, mood disorders, and in severe cases, cardiovascular events.

Diagnostic Process and Treatment

To evaluate potential health risks, patients undergo either home sleep testing or laboratory-based polysomnography. We utilize oral appliance therapy, which works by advancing the jaw and tongue position to enhance airway patency, sometimes improving nasal breathing as well. While clinical evidence supports the effectiveness of oral appliances in managing sleep-disordered breathing, individual results may vary due to factors such as nasal blockage, anatomical airway configuration, and body weight. Follow-up testing ensures treatment efficacy.

Potential Side Effects and Risks

Initial Adaptation: Some patients may experience temporary effects including enhanced saliva production, temporomandibular joint discomfort, dental sensitivity, and minor bite changes.

Long-term Considerations: Permanent occlusal changes may occur due to jaw joint or dental movement. Appliance wear and breakage is possible, with risk of swallowing or aspirating device components. Consistent nightly use is essential for OSA patients, and treatment discontinuation poses significant health risks.

Treatment Duration and Monitoring

This mechanical intervention requires lifelong nightly use for continued effectiveness. Regular biannual check-ups are mandatory to ensure proper fit and oral health maintenance. Sleep disorders may progress over time, potentially affecting treatment efficacy. Despite subjective improvements, objective sleep testing is necessary to confirm adequate oxygen levels and treatment success.

Treatment Alternatives

Available options include behavioral modifications, weight management, CPAP therapy, and surgical interventions.

Uncommon Complications

Potential rare complications include dental damage, restoration displacement, oral lesions, periodontal issues, root resorption, pulpal complications, muscular dysfunction, and auditory disturbances.
